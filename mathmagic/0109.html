Problem of the Month
(January 2009)

This month we consider a pair of problems involving chess pieces (no pawns) and polyominoes.

1. Given a polyomino P, what is the smallest number of chess pieces that can be put on a rectangular chessboard so that the unoccupied unattacked squares form P? What are the answers for some polyominoes?

2. Given a polyomino P and a chess piece, what is the smallest number of copies of P that can be arranged so that the chess piece can visit each square exactly once and return to its starting square? We insist that the copies of P be non-overlapping, and that the cycle of chess moves not leave the polyominoes. What are the answers for some polyominoes and chess pieces? Does one copy of P always suffice for queens? For each chess piece, what is the smallest polyomino that requires n copies for a cycle? Are there polyomino - chess piece pairs that are impossible?
